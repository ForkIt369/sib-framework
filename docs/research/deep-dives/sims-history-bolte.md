# The Sims: Concept / Lexicon / Vibe / Essence

> Deep dive from *The Sims* by Mari Bolte (Norwood House Press, "A Great Game!" series, 2022)

---

## Core Essence

A concise franchise-level history tracing The Sims from Will Wright's 1991 house fire epiphany through two decades of expansion into a cultural phenomenon generating $5 billion in revenue and 200 million PC copies sold. The book frames The Sims as the game that proved mundane life--chores, relationships, jobs, toilets--could be more compelling than combat, fundamentally reshaping what video games could be about. It positions the franchise not just as a product but as a creative platform and community ecosystem where players, streamers, modders, and celebrity ambassadors co-create the experience.

## Key Lexicon

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Simlish** | The invented language Sims speak; originated when voice actors struggled with real lesser-known languages (Navajo, Estonian, Ukrainian) and instead played an improv game using made-up words |
| **Simoleons** | In-game currency shared across The Sims and SimCity; symbol is the section sign (ยง); not based on any real currency |
| **Autonomous** | A Sim reacting to events or interactions on its own without player input (e.g., responding to smelly trash or a fire) |
| **Traits** | Up to three personality attributes chosen from Social, Emotional, Lifestyle, and Hobby categories that determine how a Sim thinks and acts |
| **Aspiration** | A life goal set for a Sim at creation that directs its entire existence (e.g., learning new things, making friends) |
| **Create-A-Sim** | The character creation tool offering 700+ accessories for experienced players; a tutorial/story mode exists for newcomers |
| **Custom Content (CC)** | Any game content not made by EA that adds textures to the game's graphics |
| **Maxis Match (MM)** | CC style that matches the game's native graphic style |
| **Alpha** | CC style that falls outside the game's native aesthetic, aiming for more realistic or stylistically distinct visuals |
| **Shoo Flee** | Simlish exclamation meaning "There's a problem!" |
| **Expansion Pack** | Major content additions that broaden the game world with new items, jobs, scenarios, locations |
| **Game Pack** | Mid-tier additions that add skills, abilities, and sometimes new places to visit or fantasy worlds |
| **Stuff Pack** | Smaller additions focused on outfits, hairstyles, skills, and decorations |
| **Kit** | Mini collections (throwback wardrobes, country kitchen appliances, housecleaning items, Moroccan-themed patio sets) |
| **Let's Play** | Watching someone play The Sims on Twitch or YouTube while narrating, telling jokes, or adding commentary |
| **Sims Ambassador** | Celebrity representatives chosen by EA to promote the franchise (Millie Bobby Brown, Vanessa Hudgens, HWASA, Bilal Hassani, BamBam of GOT7) |
| **The House Party** | A 2020 COVID-era collaborative Twitch event where streamers collectively built a location, created characters, and told a story in The Sims |
| **Spark'd** | A web contest reality show (TBS/Buzzfeed Multiplayer YouTube) where 12 Simmers competed for $100,000; premiered July 20, 2020 |
| **Melanin Packs** | Custom content created by Xmiramira (a Black Simmer) adding dozens of realistic skin tones and makeup options to address the game's limited darker skin representation |
| **Play With Life** | EA's marketing campaign and tagline for the franchise |
| **Lifestyle Points / Social Points** | Additional in-game currencies beyond Simoleons; Social Points can be purchased with real money or earned in-game |

## The Vibe

1. **Destruction birthed creation.** Wright made Raid on Bungeling Bay (1984), an action game about flying helicopters and blowing up factories. He discovered he preferred building the maps to destroying them. The entire Sims franchise descends from a designer who got bored with his own explosions.

2. **Loss as design catalyst.** In 1991, Wright lost his house and car to wildfires. Replacing possessions made him question why people buy things to make themselves happy. This existential inquiry about materialism, relationships, and what matters in life became the direct conceptual seed for The Sims.

3. **The "toilet game" as institutional resistance test.** The Maxis board of directors saw The Sims as an "interactive dollhouse" and couldn't believe players would want to take out trash or clean toilets. They literally called it "the toilet game." This skepticism is a recurring pattern in game industry history: the most transformative ideas face the most institutional resistance precisely because they don't fit existing categories.

4. **Chores become fun through displacement.** The book identifies a paradox at the heart of The Sims' appeal: "players love doing chores--as long as their Sim is doing it for them." This is not escapism from life but a strange mirror of it, where the mundane becomes entertaining through the act of orchestration rather than execution.

5. **Language as solved problem.** English dialogue would get boring because of limited pre-recorded responses. The first attempt was to use lesser-known real languages, but actors struggled. The solution--an improv game producing gibberish--accidentally created one of gaming's most iconic cultural artifacts (Simlish). The best design solutions sometimes emerge from failure cascades.

6. **Rejection as redirection.** Broderbund turned down SimCity because "they wanted games with a beginning and an end." Wright's open-ended, non-linear design philosophy was literally unmarketable by existing standards. EA later buying Maxis in 1997 and greenlighting "the toilet game" represents institutional courage that reshaped the industry.

7. **The community IS the product.** The book devotes an entire chapter to the community ecosystem: galleries, challenges, CC creators, Let's Play streamers, modders, reality shows, and celebrity ambassadors. The game is a platform; the community is the content engine. More than 41 million uploads and 825 million downloads in the Gallery alone demonstrate this.

8. **Representation as community-driven innovation.** Xmiramira's Melanin Packs story is telling: a Black player couldn't make Sims that looked like her, so she created the solution herself, won Spark'd, and EA subsequently announced official skin tone and hairstyle additions. The community doesn't just extend the game--it corrects it.

9. **COVID revealed the game's social infrastructure.** EA's "Stay Home, Play Together" campaign and The House Party streaming event in April 2020 showed The Sims functioning as social infrastructure during isolation. The game that simulates life became a substitute for it.

10. **The franchise as self-replicating ecosystem.** With 75+ expansion/stuff packs, 200 million copies, $5 billion in revenue, and 30 million unique players on The Sims 4 alone, the franchise operates less like a game and more like a living system that continuously regenerates through official content, community mods, streaming culture, and cross-game pollination.

## Franchise Timeline

| Date | Event |
|---|---|
| **1984** | Will Wright creates *Raid on Bungeling Bay*; sells ~1 million copies, mostly on NES in Japan |
| **1987** | Wright meets Jeff Braun at a party; Braun sells his company, Wright contributes Raid money; they co-found **Maxis Software** |
| **February 1989** | *SimCity* released; *NYT*, *Time*, and *Newsweek* give full-page reviews (first-ever computer game reviews for Time/Newsweek); over 1 million copies sold by 1992 |
| **1991** | Wright loses house and car to California wildfires; begins conceptualizing a game about "building and living a life" |
| **~1993-1999** | Maxis board resists "the toilet game"; Maxis creates SimCity sequels including *SimEarth* and *SimAnt* |
| **1997** | Electronic Arts (EA) acquires Maxis; gives Wright's team resources to finish The Sims |
| **1999** | The Sims premieres at E3 (Electronics Entertainment Expo); initially receives little attention, then word-of-mouth builds excitement |
| **February 2000** | **The Sims** released; instant success; sells 11 million copies in first two years; becomes best-selling PC game for four consecutive years |
| **August 2000** | *Livin' Large*, the first expansion pack, released; six more expansion packs follow |
| **2002** | *The Sims Online* launches as an MMO ($9.99/month); 12 different cities; players learn skills, buy/sell items |
| **2003** | *The Sims Bustin' Out* releases on PlayStation 2 with online play option (weekend getaway with up to 30 people) |
| **2004** | **The Sims 2** released; replaces The Sims as #1 selling PC game; fully 3D world; Sims experience aging and life stages; 8 expansion packs and 9 stuff packs follow |
| **2007** | *The Sims Online* renamed *EA Land*; does not recover |
| **2008** | *EA Land* (formerly The Sims Online) shut down |
| **2009** | **The Sims 3** released; Sims gain wishes, goals, more personality; 11 expansion packs and 9 stuff packs added; features include making movies, island paradises, college, seeing the future; **Will Wright leaves Maxis** |
| **2011** | *The Sims FreePlay* launches for mobile devices; real-time quest completion |
| **2014** | **The Sims 4** released; five different launch editions with exclusive items; full Sim customization; new neighborhoods and worlds |
| **March 6, 2018** | *The Sims Mobile* released for iOS and Android; based on The Sims 4; becomes third-most downloaded game in Apple App Store |
| **2018** | Millie Bobby Brown chosen as Sims Ambassador; her Sim available in EA Gallery; Positivity Challenge launched |
| **2019** | EA's Play event introduces *Island Living* expansion pack; by this year, franchise has generated **$5 billion in revenue** and **200 million copies sold on PC** |
| **January 2020** | *Tiny Living Stuff Pack* released; preceded by a Tiny Living community challenge (20-by-15 lot) |
| **February 2020** | The Sims celebrates **20th anniversary**; ~10 million active users in 2020; 30+ million unique players have created a Sim |
| **April 2020** | EA launches "Stay Home, Play Together" during COVID-19; The House Party collaborative Twitch streaming event; #SimTogether campaign |
| **July 20, 2020** | *The Sims Spark'd* reality show premieres (first episode); 12 Simmers, $100,000 prize, teams of Stylist/Builder/Storyteller |
| **August 10, 2020** | *Spark'd* final episode airs; Team Llama wins with a story about female gamers being bullied |
| **July 2021** | *Cottage Living* expansion pack released (latest at time of publication); England-inspired world with farm animals |
| **2021** | Will Wright puts out call for creators to help with new simulation game called *Proxi* |

## Design Philosophy Evolution

### Phase 1: Builder-to-Life (1984-2000)
Wright's trajectory from action game (Raid on Bungeling Bay) to city builder (SimCity) to life simulator (The Sims) represents a progressive zoom-in from macro systems to individual human experience. Each step moved closer to simulating the personal and relational rather than the structural and systemic. The 1991 wildfire was the inflection point where abstract systems thinking met personal emotional reality.

### Phase 2: Depth Through Expansion (2000-2009)
The Sims 1 through 3 show progressive deepening of simulation fidelity: from flat 2D interactions to full 3D worlds (Sims 2), from basic needs management to personality-driven wishes and goals (Sims 3). The expansion pack model--seven for Sims 1, seventeen for Sims 2, twenty for Sims 3--became the franchise's economic and creative engine, continuously broadening what "life" could mean in-game.

### Phase 3: Platform and Community (2014-present)
The Sims 4 represents a shift from game-as-product to game-as-platform. The four-tier content system (expansion packs, game packs, stuff packs, kits) creates a granular monetization and customization model. But more significantly, the Gallery (41 million uploads, 825 million downloads), CC ecosystem, Let's Play culture, and events like Spark'd show the franchise becoming a creative medium rather than just a game. EA's responsiveness to community demands (skin tones after Xmiramira's advocacy) indicates a co-evolutionary relationship between developer and player base.

### Phase 4: Ubiquity and Social Infrastructure
Mobile versions (FreePlay 2011, Sims Mobile 2018), failed online experiments (The Sims Online 2002-2008, The Sims Social on Facebook), and COVID-era social programming show ongoing attempts to make The Sims a social platform, not just a single-player sandbox. The franchise learned that players want to share creations and stories but on their own terms--the Gallery model succeeded where forced multiplayer failed.

## Cultural Impact

### By the Numbers (at 20th anniversary, 2020)
- **200 million** copies sold on PC
- **$5 billion** in franchise revenue (by 2019)
- **30+ million** unique players on The Sims 4
- **~10 million** active users in 2020
- **1.6 billion** Sims created
- **575 million** Sim households built
- **6.9 million** vampires created
- **1 million** mermaids created
- **65 million** hours spent searching the Gallery
- **41 million** Gallery uploads
- **825 million** Gallery downloads

### Genre Creation
The book explicitly states: "Without the success of SimCity and The Sims, many simulation games would not exist today." It names Animal Crossing, Harvest Moon, Second Life, Farmville, Stardew Valley, Minecraft, Paralives, and Cities: Skylines as descendants or analogues. The Sims proved that everyday life simulation was a viable, indeed dominant, game genre.

### Streaming and Content Culture
Most top Sims streamers are female. The Let's Play community keeps the game culturally visible through narrative play, challenge runs, build showcases, and mod demonstrations. The streaming ecosystem functions as both marketing and creative extension of the game itself.

### Celebrity Crossover
Sims Ambassadors (Millie Bobby Brown, Vanessa Hudgens, HWASA, Bilal Hassani, BamBam) represent EA's strategy of leveraging celebrity fandom to maintain cultural relevance across demographics and geographies. Brown cited "creativity and diversity" as reasons she plays.

### Representation and Inclusivity
The Xmiramira/Melanin Packs story is the book's most pointed cultural observation: a game about simulating "any life" initially failed to represent darker skin tones realistically. Community members created the fix; EA eventually followed. The Create-A-Sim system's progression toward full customization (walk style, physical frame, voice tone, facial features, skin tones from warm/neutral/cool/miscellaneous palettes) reflects broader cultural demands for representation in media.

### Reality TV and Competition
*The Sims Spark'd* (2020) crossed gaming into reality television, with TBS broadcast and Buzzfeed YouTube distribution. The competition format--Stylist, Builder, Storyteller roles--codified the different ways people engage with The Sims into recognizable creative disciplines.

### COVID-19 Social Function
The "Stay Home, Play Together" campaign and The House Party event positioned The Sims as social infrastructure during pandemic isolation, partnering with NFL, NBA, NHL, FIFA, and UFC. A game about simulating life became a substitute for communal life when physical gathering became impossible.

## Core Insight

The Sims franchise succeeded by inverting the fundamental assumption of game design--that players need conflict, enemies, and victory conditions to stay engaged--and replacing it with the proposition that orchestrating ordinary life (relationships, chores, careers, homes) is inherently compelling when mediated through a creative platform that players can endlessly reshape, share, and make their own.
